How Mod-7 Works
01
7-Digit Base
An AWB number starts with a 7-digit numeric base, e.g. 1234567. This is the unique shipment identifier.
02
Mod-7 Division
Divide the 7-digit number by 7. The remainder (0 to 6) becomes the check digit: 1234567 % 7 = 4
03
8-Digit AWB
Append the check digit to get the full 8-digit AWB: 12345674. Any carrier can verify this instantly.

Why Mod-7?
Not Mod-10

The algorithm choice behind Air Waybill check digits and why it still holds up.

MOD 7 -- AWB
serial % 7 = check digit
Fast · Simple · Legacy-safe
vs
MOD 10 -- Luhn
weighted digit sum
Accurate · Complex · Banking
01
Legacy System Constraints
Air cargo systems were built on mainframes running COBOL with limited compute and unreliable networks. Mod-7 is a single division operation, exactly what those environments needed.
02
Speed Over Perfection
Cargo operations need fast, high-throughput validation. Mod-7 is nearly instantaneous. Luhn's weighted calculation is overkill when processing thousands of shipments per minute.
03
Different Error Profile
Banking needs fraud prevention so Luhn is essential. Air cargo errors are mostly typos and data entry mistakes. Mod-7 catches those well enough. The risk profile does not justify the complexity.
04
IATA Global Lock-in
Once IATA standardised Mod-7, millions of systems worldwide adopted it. Even if Mod-10 were better, the switching cost far exceeds any benefit.
⚡
The Brutal Takeaway
Mod-7 was not chosen because it is the best algorithm. It was chosen because it is the cheapest algorithm that solves the problem well enough at global scale.
